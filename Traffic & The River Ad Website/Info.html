Traffic & The River is a homemade video pack independently developed by me (Gregory Desrosiers) based on a YouTube video called “Amazon Rainforest Relaxation Video,” one of the first videos uploaded on what eventually became the world’s biggest video sharing websites. It was uploaded and filmed by Tom Lynskey (director and writer of his latest film, The Last Signals, http://www.youtube.com/user/jkilts) with music by Klaus Back and Tini Beier, uploaded on October 17, 2006.

Experience the full potential of the pack with:

  1. shots recorded at 720p Fine (42 minutes)
  2. Enhanced Sony Vegas Movie Studio HD visual effects
  3. the sharpest quality possible from the Windows Media Video compressor
  4. HD resolution (1920 x 1080, progressive scan)
  5. 29.97 frames per second (avoiding ragged frames from camera shakes)
  6. transitions for smooth and logical flow
  7. Orchestrated music composed to follow the flow of the videos
  8. Audio bitrate at 320 kbps with sampling rate at 44,100 Hz, stereo (CD audio)

Traffic, if you are a technical person, explains how far we could invent machines and how much we use them for us to earn freedom. Just the word “techno” doesn’t come far enough from original camera shots. Each and every clip is saturated with a possible combination of color adjusters, some hard to even develop on your own, light adjusters and several shapes and styles of frames. For example, a few seconds can have lots of solid-color squares in place of random pixels from the shot.

The River is more than just looking at a river. You get to see plants that are on the other side. You take a look at some gravel and soil shores that you can throw rocks or pebbles in the water for fun. You even see calm waters with plants having their leaves and green parts glide into the wind. There are no parts that are man-made. Every frame of the video is condensed to focus only on Mother Nature and what words it and the music speak out. Calm yourself into the world and you get to feel the sense of what it’s like to be in a natural environment with no human footprint.

As part of the project, I’m planning on publicizing the pack to students at Champlain and Dawson. Because of how I’m now getting a composer from the classified sites of McGill and Concordia, I should consider publicizing the pack to some enthusiasts over there. This could potentially become a show in which I’ll have to team up with several administrative people and form a contract with the council of Montreal. For now, the progress is being managed by Champlain’s SLAM Life Manager, Dave Persons, a member of the college’s Student Services team that organize events for the college.

I’m planning to finish the music videos by late March, because organizing a potential show could take several weeks. The latest time we can premiere the show is May 25th, officially the last day of exams. After this, I will sell copies of the pack to the general public through eBay and Amazon.
